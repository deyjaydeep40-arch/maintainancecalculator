What research says about dowry in India

Population-level findings from published economic and demographic research — not a personal calculator. These studies describe historical and statistical patterns across large samples; they say nothing about any individual case, and none of them should be read as a guide to what dowry "should" be.

Education & dowry size

Higher groom education tracks with higher dowries — but not the way people assume

Research on Indian marriage markets found dowry amounts rise with a groom's absolute level of education, not his ranking relative to peers of the same caste. But as more men reach a given education level, the dowry premium for that credential shrinks — because he's no longer as distinctive among his peers.

Source: Chiplunkar & Weaver, Marriage Markets and the Rise of Dowry in India, IZA Discussion Paper 16135

National trend data

Average recorded net dowry, most recent nationwide rural data

Using a 2006 survey covering roughly 96% of India's population, researchers found the average bride's family transferred about seven times more in gifts than they received back from the groom's family — an average net dowry of roughly ₹27,000 in real terms. Dowry amounts declined after 1975 but began rising again after 2000.

Source: World Bank, The evolution of dowry in rural India: 1960–2008

Household wealth

Wealth predicts whether dowry is paid more reliably than any single factor

A study of over 5,000 married adolescent girls in Uttar Pradesh and Bihar found those from the wealthiest households were roughly 48% more likely to have had dowry paid on their behalf than those from poorer households. Dowry was also significantly less common in marriages where the couple knew each other beforehand, compared with fully arranged matches.

Source: Banned by the law, practiced by the society, PMC / UDAYA project data

Caste patterns

Caste hierarchy in dowry amounts has stayed remarkably stable

The same nationwide rural dataset found dowry positively correlated with higher caste status — and that this relative ordering between caste groups had not meaningfully shifted across the decades studied, even as absolute dowry amounts rose and fell.

Source: World Bank, The evolution of dowry in rural India: 1960–2008

Why there's no calculator here: these figures describe averages across large, historical survey samples — they cannot and should not be used to estimate what any individual should pay, demand, or accept. Doing either is a criminal offence under the Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, regardless of the numbers involved.
